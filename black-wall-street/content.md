# A Map You Can Read Like a Story

label: Opening

This project rebuilds a business landscape that is easy to miss if you only read the archive. Here, addresses become places, and places become a way to see how Black life was organized in Roxbury across the first half of the twentieth century. Mapping makes patterns visible that the archive alone cannot show, including clustering, adjacency, and persistence across policy boundaries.

> Scroll to move through the narrative. The map changes as each section comes into view.

:::chapter{id="02"}
# Project overview

label: What you are looking at

This project reconstructs the spatial history of Black owned businesses in Roxbury, Boston between 1900 and 1960. It turns scattered archival listings into a street level record of Black enterprise, showing where businesses clustered, how they related to major corridors, and how they persisted through changing policy and investment patterns. The map is designed to be explored layer by layer, so the story can unfold in place.
:::

:::chapter{id="05"}
# 1914 directory points

label: Archival evidence

In the 1914 Directory of Negro Business and Professional Men and Women of Boston, Elmira Williams framed the publication as a response to claims that Black Americans could not be economically self-supporting, emphasizing the breadth of enterprise in Boston. Building on this historical record, business names and addresses were manually extracted, cleaned, standardized, and geocoded in ArcGIS Pro to reconstruct Black commercial life in Roxbury, recognizing that directories provide a substantial but partial archive. For example, Johnson Hair Store trained Black women in cosmetology, yet in 1920 its founder, Mary Johnson, was registered to vote in Boston while still recorded as a housewife, illustrating the layered economic and social identities of Black women in the early twentieth century.

:::chapter{id="06"}
# 1915 National Negro Business League listings

label: Archival evidence

The 1915 National Negro Business League Souvenir Program documented a vibrant network of Black entrepreneurs and professionals asserting economic self-determination in an era of racial exclusion. Published during the height of Jim Crow segregation, it reflected a broader movement of institution-building and collective economic advancement within African American communities. In the same historical moment, Old North Church's Black community represented a long-standing yet often segregated presence in Boston's religious life, shaped by national struggles for spiritual autonomy and civil rights. When mapped, the business listings from the program reveal the spatial concentration of Black commercial activity and its connection to neighborhood circulation routes, illustrating how economic and religious histories intersected within the city's urban fabric.

:::

:::chapter{id="07"}
# Green Book listings

label: Archival evidence

The Negro Motorist Green Book, published from 1936 to 1966 by Victor Hugo Green, guided African American travelers to safe and welcoming businesses during the Jim Crow era, becoming an essential tool for navigating segregation and racial violence. Though it ceased publication after the Civil Rights Act of 1964, it remains a lasting symbol of resilience, mobility, and collective self-reliance. Example is the Harriet Tubman House, founded in 1904 by six Black women, began as a refuge for Black women migrating to Boston. It later evolved into a vital center for social services in the South End. Through its expansion, merger into United South End Settlements, and the construction of a new facility in 1975, it became a cornerstone of community organizing, cultural life, and support for marginalized residents.

> Deep zoom: [Harriet Tubman House - 25 Holyoke St](#){.zoom-link data-poi="poi-greenbook-tubman"}
:::

:::chapter{id="08"}
# 1921 concert program and community sponsors

label: Culture and commerce

The 1921 souvenir program documents a concert held at Ebenezer Baptist Church in Boston under the auspices of the Sedalia Club, featuring Florence Cole Talbert and Louia V. Jones. The performance was organized as a benefit for the Palmer Memorial Institute in North Carolina, a school dedicated to the education and industrial training of Black youth in the rural South. In addition to outlining the musical program, the booklet includes appeals for support and advertisements from Black-owned businesses and community institutions in Boston. Together, these elements reveal the program as both a cultural artifact and a testament to Black philanthropy, entrepreneurship, and community-building in early twentieth-century Boston. Example of such businesses that was advertised in the document was the Hattie B. Cooper Community Club - 36 Williams St.

:::chapter{id="09"}
# Redlining overlays

label: Policy context

To contextualize business locations, the map includes 1930s Home Owners' Loan Corporation redlining maps. HOLC grades ran from A, described as best, to D, described as hazardous, and these classifications shaped where mortgage capital flowed. When overlaid with HOLC classifications, many businesses appear within areas later graded C or D. Seeing businesses inside these zones highlights not absence, but persistence and place making under constraint, as commercial life continued even as institutional investment was restricted.
:::

:::chapter{id="10"}
# Clusters, corridors, and community resistance

label: Findings

When the layers are brought together, a clear pattern emerges. Black owned businesses were not scattered randomly across Roxbury. They clustered along key commercial corridors, especially Tremont Street and Columbus Avenue, and around civic centers such as Dudley Square. These clusters functioned as more than marketplaces. They were community infrastructure, places where employment, credit, information, culture, and care circulated through everyday encounters.

Seen against the redlining layer, the density of these corridors is also evidence of endurance. Many businesses operated in areas marked for disinvestment, yet they continued to build local networks and public life. The map makes that resistance visible, not as a single moment, but as a long practice of staying, organizing, and creating place within a city that repeatedly tried to narrow Black opportunity.

:::

:::chapter{id="11"}
# Why mapping matters

label: Interpretation

This project reframes Black entrepreneurship not as peripheral urban activity but as a sustained and spatially embedded practice of community making. GIS functions here as a medium of historical interpretation, reconnecting names, addresses, and streets. The map does more than visualize data. It restores Black business districts to the landscape and repositions them within Boston's urban history. This map is meant to be explored, but also remembered, as a public record of enterprises that helped Black Roxbury hold together.
